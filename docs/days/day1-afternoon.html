Capital Investment Decision-Making

This afternoon, we build upon the financial framework and ratio analysis covered this morning to focus on one of the most critical aspects of financial management: capital investment decisions.

Learning Outcomes for This Session By the end of this afternoon, you will be able to:

  1. Explain the nature and importance of investment decision-making
  2. Identify and evaluate the five main investment appraisal methods found in practice
  3. Use these methods to reach decisions on investment opportunities
  4. Explain the key stages in the investment decision-making process
  5. Apply techniques to handle risk in investment decisions

The Nature of Investment Decisions

Investment decisions represent long-term commitments of substantial resources with uncertain returns. They are among the most consequential decisions finance managers must make.

Key Characteristics

Capital investment decisions typically involve:

  • Substantial resource allocation
  • Extended time horizons
  • Limited reversibility once undertaken
  • Strategic implications for the organisation’s future direction
  • Multiple risk dimensions

Real-World Example

The pharmaceutical industry provides a classic example of the capital investment decision challenge:

  • Initial R&D investment: £500m+ (significant upfront capital)
  • Development timeline: 10-15 years (extended time commitment)
  • Success rate: Only 1 in 10 compounds receives regulatory approval (substantial uncertainty)
  • Strategic importance: Defines company product pipeline for decades
  • Market conditions: Subject to change during the development period

Cash Flows vs. Accounting Profits

A fundamental principle in capital investment appraisal is the focus on cash flows rather than accounting profits.

Understanding the Difference

Cash flow definition
A cash flow represents the actual movement of money into or out of a business. It represents tangible and objective monetary transactions.

Accounting profit definition
Accounting profit is a calculated figure based on matching revenues with expenses according to accounting principles. It includes non-cash items like depreciation.

Key Equation:

Cash Flow = Profit + Depreciation + Other non-cash adjustments

Component explanation: - Begin with the accounting profit figure - Add back depreciation (which was subtracted to calculate profit but didn’t involve actual cash outflow) - Add/subtract other non-cash items (like provisions or unrealized gains/losses)

Practical example: Consider a delivery van purchased for a business at £30,000. In accounting terms, this might be depreciated at £6,000 annually for 5 years. However, in cash flow terms, the entire £30,000 outflow occurs at purchase. In subsequent years, the accounting profit shows the £6,000 depreciation expense, but the cash flow doesn’t reflect this—the cash outflow has already occurred.

Why Focus on Cash Flows?

  • Objectivity: Cash represents a measurable, tangible asset
  • Accounting variability: Profits can differ based on accounting methods selected
  • Timing implications: When cash flows occur directly impacts their value
  • Operational impact: Investment decisions directly affect cash balances, influencing an organization’s operational capabilities

Relevant Cash Flows Are:

  • Incremental: Cash flows arising directly because of the project
  • Future-oriented: Past or “sunk” costs are irrelevant
  • After-tax: Tax implications affect the actual cash benefit

Irrelevant Items Include:

  • Sunk costs: Previously committed expenditures that cannot be recovered
  • Non-incremental overhead allocations: Fixed costs that would exist regardless of the new project
  • Interest payments: These are captured in the discount rate used in evaluation
  • Accounting conventions: Such as depreciation, which don’t represent actual cash movements

Common Error to Avoid

Including already committed (sunk) costs in investment appraisal calculations. Once spent, these costs are irrelevant to the decision to proceed.

Example: If £50,000 has been spent on market research for a potential product, that £50,000 should not be included when deciding whether to launch the product. That expenditure is unrecoverable regardless of the decision.

A Practical Glossary of Key Terms

Before exploring investment appraisal methods, it’s useful to clarify some key terminology:

  • Capital investment: The allocation of funds to long-term assets or projects expected to generate value over multiple years
  • Cash flow: The actual movement of money into or out of a business
  • Discount rate: The rate used to convert future cash flows to present value, reflecting the time value of money and risk
  • Present value: The current worth of a future cash flow, adjusted for the time value of money
  • Time value of money: The concept that money available now is worth more than the same amount in the future due to its potential to earn returns
  • Incremental cash flow: Additional cash flow occurring as a direct result of a decision
  • Sunk cost: A cost already incurred that cannot be recovered
  • Opportunity cost: The value of the next best alternative foregone when making a decision
  • Terminal value: The estimated value of an asset or project at the end of a specific period
  • Risk premium: The additional return expected for accepting additional risk

Understanding the Time Value of Money Principle

Before exploring discounted cash flow methods, it’s essential to understand the time value of money.

The principle: £100 today is worth more than £100 received in one year’s time.

Reasons: 1. Opportunity Cost: Present funds can be invested to generate returns 2. Inflation: The purchasing power of money typically decreases over time 3. Uncertainty: Future cash receipts carry inherent uncertainty

Example: If funds can be invested at 5% annual interest, then: - £100 today would grow to £105 in one year (£100 × 1.05) - Therefore, £105 received one year from now is equivalent to having £100 today - The “present value” of £105 received in one year is £100

This is calculated as: £105 ÷ 1.05 = £100

Extension of this concept: - £100 today would grow to £110.25 in two years (£100 × 1.05 × 1.05) - Therefore, £110.25 received two years from now is equivalent to having £100 today - The present value of £110.25 received in two years is £100

This is calculated as: £110.25 ÷ (1.05)² = £110.25 ÷ 1.1025 = £100

DCF methods explicitly account for this through discounting.

Payback Period (PP)

The Payback Period is the time required to recover the initial investment from project cash flows.

Definition & Calculation

The Payback Period tells us the duration required for cumulative cash inflows to equal the initial investment.

Formula:

Payback Period = Time until ∑ Cash Flows = 0

In methodological terms: Calculate the cumulative cash flows (starting with the negative initial investment) until the total becomes positive. The point at which this occurs is the payback period.

Decision Rule: - Accept projects with PP < maximum acceptable period - Between competing projects, prefer shorter payback - The “maximum acceptable period” is typically established by organizational policy

Step-by-Step Example

A project costs £500,000 with the following expected cash flows:

Year Cash Flow (£) Cumulative Cash Flow (£)
0 -500,000 -500,000
1 150,000 -350,000
2 180,000 -170,000
3 200,000 30,000
4 120,000 150,000
5 100,000 250,000

Step 1: Begin with the initial investment as a negative cash flow (Year 0)

Step 2: Add each year’s cash flow to calculate a running total

Step 3: Determine when the cumulative total becomes positive (between Years 2 and 3)

Step 4: Calculate the precise payback period: - At the end of Year 2, £170,000 remains to be recovered - Year 3 generates £200,000 - The proportion of Year 3 needed is: £170,000 ÷ £200,000 = 0.85 - Therefore, Payback Period = 2 + 0.85 = 2.85 years

                         Payback Point (2.85 years)

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages: - Computational simplicity - Focus on liquidity and capital recovery - Basic risk indicator - Practical application frequency

Limitations: - Disregards cash flows beyond the payback period - Neglects time value of money - Fails to consider investment scale - Often employs arbitrary thresholds - Not aligned with shareholder wealth maximization

Discounted Payback Period (DPP)

The Discounted Payback Period addresses a key limitation of the standard payback by considering the time value of money.

Definition & Calculation

The Discounted Payback Period indicates the duration required for cumulative discounted cash inflows to equal the initial investment.

Formula:

Discounted Payback Period = Time until ∑ [Cash Flow_t / (1+r)^t] = 0

Where: - t = time period (typically years) - r = discount rate - The notation (1+r)^t means (1+r) raised to the power of t

Methodological process: 1. Convert all future cash flows to their present value (by discounting with factor (1+r)^t) 2. Calculate the cumulative discounted cash flows (starting with the initial investment) 3. The point at which the cumulative total becomes positive is the discounted payback period

Decision Rule: - Accept projects with DPP < maximum acceptable period - Between competing projects, prefer shorter discounted payback

Understanding the Discounting Process

Before proceeding to the example, it’s important to understand the discounting calculation:

Discount factor: The multiplier applied to future cash flows to determine present value

For a discount rate of 10%: - Year 1 discount factor: 1 ÷ (1+0.10)¹ = 1 ÷ 1.10 = 0.909 - Year 2 discount factor: 1 ÷ (1+0.10)² = 1 ÷ 1.21 = 0.826 - Year 3 discount factor: 1 ÷ (1+0.10)³ = 1 ÷ 1.331 = 0.751 - And so on…

Graphical representation of discount factors over time:

1.000 |*
0.909 | *
0.826 |  *
0.751 |   *
0.683 |    *
0.621 |     *
      +------
      0 1 2 3 4 5
         Years

The discount factor decreases as time extends further into the future, reflecting the diminishing present value of future cash flows.

Step-by-Step Example

Using the same project with a 10% discount rate:

Year Cash Flow (£) Discount Factor Discounted CF (£) Cumulative DCF (£)
0 -500,000 1.000 -500,000 -500,000
1 150,000 0.909 136,350 -363,650
2 180,000 0.826 148,680 -214,970
3 200,000 0.751 150,200 -64,770
4 120,000 0.683 81,960 17,190
5 100,000 0.621 62,100 79,290

Step 1: Calculate the discount factor for each year using the formula 1/(1+r)^t - Year 1: 1/(1.10)¹ = 0.909 - Year 2: 1/(1.10)² = 0.826 - And so on…

Step 2: Calculate the discounted cash flow by multiplying each cash flow by its discount factor - Year 0: -£500,000 × 1.000 = -£500,000 - Year 1: £150,000 × 0.909 = £136,350 - And so on…

Step 3: Calculate the cumulative discounted cash flow - Year 0: -£500,000 - Year 1: -£500,000 + £136,350 = -£363,650 - And so on…

Step 4: Determine when the cumulative total becomes positive (between Years 3 and 4)

Step 5: Calculate the precise discounted payback period: - At the end of Year 3, £64,770 remains to be recovered - Year 4 generates a discounted cash flow of £81,960 - The proportion of Year 4 required is: £64,770 ÷ £81,960 = 0.79 - Therefore, Discounted Payback Period = 3 + 0.79 = 3.79 years

Note: The calculation in the original table showed 3.20 years, but our worked example shows 3.79 years. For teaching purposes, we’ll use our calculated value of 3.79 years.

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages: - Incorporates time value of money: Unlike standard PP - Maintains interpretative simplicity: Still provides a clear metric for capital recovery - More accurate measure of recovery period: Based on the actual value of money over time

Limitations: - Still disregards cash flows after the payback period: May miss substantial future returns - Arbitrary threshold selection: The “acceptable” discounted payback period often lacks objective basis - Not directly linked to shareholder wealth maximization: Doesn’t necessarily indicate value creation

Net Present Value (NPV)

The Net Present Value method is the most theoretically sound approach to investment appraisal, directly linking to shareholder wealth maximization.

Definition & Calculation

Net Present Value represents the sum of all project cash flows, discounted to present value.

Formula:

NPV = ∑ [Cash Flow_t / (1+r)^t]

Where: - t = time period (typically years) - r = discount rate (cost of capital) - ∑ symbol means “sum of all terms”

Methodological process: 1. Convert all cash flows to present value using the appropriate discount rate 2. Sum all discounted cash flows (including the initial investment) 3. The resulting figure represents the net value created by the project in present terms

Decision Rule: - Accept projects with NPV > 0 (project creates value) - Reject projects with NPV < 0 (project destroys value) - Between competing projects, prefer higher NPV

Understanding the Discount Rate

The discount rate used in NPV calculations is crucial and reflects several components:

Discount rate components:
- Risk-free rate (time value of money)
- Inflation premium
- Risk premium

Risk-free rate: The return available on risk-free investments (typically government securities)

Inflation premium: Adjustment for expected decrease in purchasing power

Risk premium: Additional return required to compensate for project-specific risk

Example: - Risk-free rate: 2% - Expected inflation: 3% - Project risk premium: 5% - Resulting discount rate: 2% + 3% + 5% = 10%

The discount rate effectively establishes the minimum acceptable return for the project.

Step-by-Step Example

Using our same project with a 10% discount rate:

Year Cash Flow (£) Discount Factor Discounted CF (£)
0 -500,000 1.000 -500,000
1 150,000 0.909 136,350
2 180,000 0.826 148,680
3 200,000 0.751 150,200
4 120,000 0.683 81,960
5 100,000 0.621 62,100

Step 1: Calculate the discount factor for each year using the formula 1/(1+r)^t - Year 0: 1/(1.10)⁰ = 1.000 (no discounting for present money) - Year 1: 1/(1.10)¹ = 0.909 - Year 2: 1/(1.10)² = 0.826 - And so on…

Step 2: Calculate the discounted cash flow by multiplying each cash flow by its discount factor - Year 0: -£500,000 × 1.000 = -£500,000 - Year 1: £150,000 × 0.909 = £136,350 - And so on…

Step 3: Sum all the discounted cash flows to derive the NPV - NPV = -£500,000 + £136,350 + £148,680 + £150,200 + £81,960 + £62,100 = £79,290

Since the NPV is positive (£79,290), the project creates value and should be accepted.

Interpretative Framework for NPV

NPV interpretation: - If NPV = £79,290, the project generates £79,290 more value in present terms than an alternative investment at the discount rate - It represents the additional value created beyond the required return

Alternative perspective: - If presented with two options: - Deposit £500,000 in an investment yielding 10% annually, or - Invest £500,000 in this project - The positive NPV of £79,290 indicates the project delivers greater value than the alternative investment

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages: - Considers all cash flows over project life: No arbitrary cutoff point - Incorporates time value of money: Appropriately values future cash flows - Directly linked to shareholder wealth maximization: Positive NPV indicates increased shareholder value - Allows for risk adjustment via discount rate: Higher-risk projects can be evaluated with higher discount rates - Additive across projects: NPVs of multiple projects can be aggregated

Limitations: - Greater computational complexity: Requires understanding of discounting methods - Sensitivity to discount rate selection: Minor changes in discount rate can significantly impact NPV - Assumes reinvestment at the discount rate: May not always reflect reality - Provides absolute rather than relative measure: Doesn’t indicate capital efficiency

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The Internal Rate of Return is the discount rate at which a project’s NPV equals zero, representing the project’s “yield.”

Definition & Calculation

The Internal Rate of Return represents the discount rate that equates the present value of cash inflows with the present value of cash outflows.

Formula: IRR is the value of r that satisfies:

∑ [Cash Flow_t / (1+r)^t] = 0

Methodological process: 1. Determine the discount rate at which NPV equals exactly zero 2. This rate represents the project’s effective return rate 3. At this exact rate, the investment would be financially neutral

Decision Rule: - Accept projects with IRR > cost of capital - Reject projects with IRR < cost of capital - Between competing projects, prefer higher IRR

Understanding IRR Conceptually

IRR interpretation: - Represents the project’s internal efficiency or yield - Comparable to an effective interest rate on the investment - If IRR exceeds the cost of capital, the project creates value

Mathematical solution: - Unlike NPV, no direct formula exists to calculate IRR - Requires iterative calculation or financial software - Solves for the discount rate that results in NPV = 0

Step-by-Step Example

Using the same project cash flows:

Year Cash Flow (£)
0 -500,000
1 150,000
2 180,000
3 200,000
4 120,000
5 100,000

To find the IRR, we need to determine the value of r where:

-500,000 + 150,000/(1+r)¹ + 180,000/(1+r)² + 200,000/(1+r)³ + 120,000/(1+r)⁴ + 100,000/(1+r)⁵ = 0

Iterative Approach: 1. Begin with r = 15% - NPV = -£36,764 (negative) 2. Try r = 12% - NPV = £13,477 (positive) 3. Try r = 14% - NPV = -£16,025 (negative) 4. Try r = 13% - NPV = -£2,048 (negative) 5. Try r = 12.5% - NPV = £5,477 (positive) 6. Try r = 12.8% - NPV = £560 (positive) 7. Try r = 12.9% - NPV = -£1,088 (negative)

Through this iterative process, we can estimate that IRR ≈ 12.85%

Since IRR (12.85%) > Cost of capital (10%), the project is acceptable.

Key insights from this graph: - At discount rates below IRR, NPV is positive - At discount rates above IRR, NPV is negative - At exactly IRR, NPV is zero

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages: - Percentage format aids interpretation: Managers often find percentage returns more intuitive than absolute values - Incorporates time value of money: Appropriately values future cash flows - Considers complete cash flow profile: Includes all project cash flows - Widespread professional application: Commonly used in business contexts

Limitations: - Multiple IRRs possible with non-conventional cash flows: If cash flows change direction more than once - Scale of investment not considered: A smaller project might have a higher IRR but create less total value - Reinvestment assumption may be unrealistic: Assumes cash flows can be reinvested at the IRR - Potential ranking conflicts with NPV: May produce different project rankings than NPV - Indirect relationship to shareholder wealth maximization: A high IRR doesn’t necessarily maximize value

Real-World Example: When IRR and NPV Conflict

Consider two mutually exclusive projects:

Project Initial Investment Cash Flow Year 1 IRR NPV (at 10%)
Small -£10,000 £13,000 30% £1,818
Large -£100,000 £115,000 15% £4,545

IRR Ranking: Small Project (30%) > Large Project (15%) NPV Ranking: Large Project (£4,545) > Small Project (£1,818)

Analysis: NPV provides the better guide to value creation. The large project creates more total wealth, even though its percentage return is lower.

Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

The Accounting Rate of Return focuses on accounting profit rather than cash flows.

Definition & Calculation

The Accounting Rate of Return tells us: “What is the average accounting return on the investment?”

Formula:

ARR = (Average annual profit / Average investment) × 100%

Where: - Average annual profit = Total project profits ÷ Project life - Average investment = (Initial investment + Residual value) ÷ 2

In plain English: 1. Calculate the average annual accounting profit over the project’s life 2. Calculate the average investment over the project’s life 3. Divide the average profit by the average investment to get the return rate

Decision Rule: - Accept projects with ARR > minimum required return - Reject projects with ARR < minimum required return - Between competing projects, prefer higher ARR

Understanding the Difference from Cash Flow Methods

Unlike NPV and IRR, ARR uses accounting profits, which: - Include non-cash items like depreciation - Follow accounting conventions that may vary between companies - Don’t consider the timing of cash flows

This makes it fundamentally different from discounted cash flow methods.

Step-by-Step Example

A project requires an initial investment of £500,000 with the following profit projections:

Year Accounting Profit (£)
1 90,000
2 110,000
3 130,000
4 80,000
5 70,000

With a residual value of £100,000.

Step 1: Calculate the total and average annual profit - Total profit = £90,000 + £110,000 + £130,000 + £80,000 + £70,000 = £480,000 - Average annual profit = £480,000 ÷ 5 = £96,000

Step 2: Calculate the average investment - Initial investment = £500,000 - Residual value = £100,000 - Average investment = (£500,000 + £100,000) ÷ 2 = £300,000

Step 3: Calculate the ARR - ARR = (£96,000 ÷ £300,000) × 100% = 32%

Note: The ARR of 32% cannot be directly compared with the IRR of 12.85% from our previous example, as they measure different things.

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages: - Uses familiar accounting concepts: No need to convert to cash flows - Simple to calculate: Basic arithmetic only - Addresses profitability directly: Based on accounting profit, which is familiar to managers - No discounting required: Avoids complexity of time value calculations

Limitations: - Based on accounting profits, not cash flows: May not reflect actual cash generation - Ignores time value of money: Treats profits in Year 1 the same as profits in Year 5 - No objective benchmark for minimum ARR: What’s a “good” ARR is often arbitrary - Sensitive to accounting policies: Different depreciation methods can change the ARR - Multiple calculation methods exist: Different companies may calculate it differently

Comparing Appraisal Methods Using a Simple Example

Let’s use a very simple example to see how each method evaluates a project:

Project X: Initial investment of £1,000 with the following cash flows: - Year 1: £500 - Year 2: £600 - Year 3: £400

Cost of capital (discount rate): 10%

Payback Period (PP)

Initial investment: £1,000 - Year 1: £500 (Cumulative: £500) - Year 2: £600 (Cumulative: £1,100)

Payback Period = 1 + (£1,000 - £500)/£600 = 1 + 0.83 = 1.83 years

Discounted Payback Period (DPP)

Initial investment: £1,000 - Year 1: £500 × 0.909 = £454.50 (Cumulative: £454.50) - Year 2: £600 × 0.826 = £495.60 (Cumulative: £950.10) - Year 3: £400 × 0.751 = £300.40 (Cumulative: £1,250.50)

Discounted Payback Period = 2 + (£1,000 - £950.10)/£300.40 = 2 + 0.17 = 2.17 years

Net Present Value (NPV)

NPV = -£1,000 + £454.50 + £495.60 + £300.40 = £250.50

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Finding the discount rate where: -£1,000 + £500/(1+r)¹ + £600/(1+r)² + £400/(1+r)³ = 0

Using financial calculator: IRR = 22.1%

Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

Assuming straight-line depreciation over 3 years and zero residual value: - Annual depreciation = £1,000 ÷ 3 = £333.33 - Accounting profits: - Year 1: £500 - £333.33 = £166.67 - Year 2: £600 - £333.33 = £266.67 - Year 3: £400 - £333.33 = £66.67 - Average annual profit = (£166.67 + £266.67 + £66.67) ÷ 3 = £166.67 - Average investment = (£1,000 + £0) ÷ 2 = £500 - ARR = (£166.67 ÷ £500) × 100% = 33.3%

Best Practice

Organizations typically use multiple appraisal methods in combination to gain a more complete perspective on potential investments.

Which Method Should You Use?

For maximizing shareholder value: NPV is theoretically the best method as it directly measures value creation

For comparing projects of different sizes: NPV for total value created, IRR for efficiency of capital use

For communicating with non-financial managers: Payback Period and IRR are more intuitive

For quick screening of proposals: Payback Period can be calculated easily to filter out poor investments

For comprehensiveness: Use multiple methods to get different perspectives on the investment

Case Study: Evaluating a New Business Venture

Let’s apply these methods to a real-world case:

Evaluating the Potential of Starting a Plumbing Business

Business Plan Overview

The proposal involves:

  • Year 0: Initial investment (Equipment: £25,000, Van: £15,000, Advertising: £5,000)
  • Years 1-5: Operations (Building customer base, hiring employees)
  • End of Year 5: Exit strategy (Sell business for £30,000, equipment value £8,000, van value £3,000)

Expected cash flows: - Year 1: £12,000 - Year 2: £18,000 - Year 3: £22,000 - Year 4: £25,000 - Year 5: £26,000 (plus exit values)

Applying PP and DPP

Year Cash Flow (£) Cumulative CF (£) Discount Factor Discounted CF (£) Cumulative DCF (£)
0 -45,000 -45,000 1.000 -45,000 -45,000
1 12,000 -33,000 0.909 10,908 -34,092
2 18,000 -15,000 0.826 14,868 -19,224
3 22,000 7,000 0.751 16,522 -2,702
4 25,000 32,000 0.683 17,075 14,373
5 67,000 99,000 0.621 41,607 55,980

Calculating Payback Period: - At the end of Year 2, we’re still £15,000 short of breaking even - In Year 3, we generate £22,000 - The fraction of Year 3 needed is: £15,000 ÷ £22,000 = 0.68 - Therefore, Payback Period = 2 + 0.68 = 2.68 years

Calculating Discounted Payback Period: - At the end of Year 3, we’re still £2,702 short of breaking even - In Year 4, we generate discounted cash flow of £17,075 - The fraction of Year 4 needed is: £2,702 ÷ £17,075 = 0.16 - Therefore, Discounted Payback Period = 3 + 0.16 = 3.16 years

Calculating NPV and IRR

Net Present Value (NPV): Sum of all discounted cash flows = £55,980

Internal Rate of Return (IRR): Finding the discount rate where: -£45,000 + £12,000/(1+r)¹ + £18,000/(1+r)² + £22,000/(1+r)³ + £25,000/(1+r)⁴ + £67,000/(1+r)⁵ = 0

Using financial calculator: IRR = 33.5%

Calculating ARR

Year Cash Flow (£) Depreciation (£) Accounting Profit (£)
1 12,000 800 11,200
2 18,000 800 17,200
3 22,000 800 21,200
4 25,000 800 24,200
5 26,000 800 25,200

Step 1: Calculate depreciation (assuming straight-line) - Total value to depreciate = Initial investment - Residual value = £45,000 - £41,000 = £4,000 - Annual depreciation = £4,000 ÷ 5 = £800

Step 2: Calculate accounting profits by subtracting depreciation from cash flows

Step 3: Calculate average annual profit - Total accounting profit = £11,200 + £17,200 + £21,200 + £24,200 + £25,200 = £99,000 - Average annual profit = £99,000 ÷ 5 = £19,800

Step 4: Calculate average investment - Average investment = (£45,000 + £41,000) ÷ 2 = £43,000

Step 5: Calculate ARR - ARR = (£19,800 ÷ £43,000) × 100% = 46.0%

Decision Recommendation

Based on all five appraisal methods, we can make the following assessment:

  1. Payback Period (2.68 years): If the maximum acceptable payback is 3 years, the project is acceptable.

  2. Discounted Payback (3.16 years): If the maximum acceptable discounted payback is 4 years, the project is acceptable.

  3. NPV (£55,980): The positive NPV indicates the project adds substantial value to the business and should be accepted.

  4. IRR (33.5%): If the cost of capital is 10%, the project’s IRR substantially exceeds this threshold and should be accepted.

  5. ARR (46.0%): If the minimum required ARR is 15%, the project is well above this threshold and acceptable.

Recommendation: The investment in the plumbing business appears financially viable across all appraisal methods. The positive NPV and high IRR are particularly encouraging indicators of value creation.

Sensitivity Analysis

Sensitivity analysis examines how changes in key variables affect project outcomes.

Process: 1. Identify key variables (e.g., sales volume, price, costs) 2. Calculate impact of percentage changes in each variable 3. Identify switching points where NPV becomes zero 4. Focus management attention on most sensitive variables

Example: For our plumbing business, how much would each variable need to change to make NPV zero?

Variable Base Value Change for Zero NPV Interpretation
Initial investment £45,000 +124.4% Low sensitivity
Annual cash flows As projected -43.7% Moderate sensitivity
Exit value £41,000 -136.5% Low sensitivity
Discount rate 10% +23.5 percentage points Low sensitivity

Interpreting the results: - The project’s NPV would become zero if: - Initial investment increased by 124.4% (to £100,980) - Annual cash flows decreased by 43.7% (nearly halved) - Exit value decreased by 136.5% (becoming negative, which is unrealistic) - Discount rate increased to 33.5% (from 10%)

Since most of these changes are extreme, the project appears robust to reasonable variations.

Risk Attitudes

Different investors have different risk preferences:

  • Risk-averse investors: Require compensation for bearing risk
  • Risk-neutral investors: Focus solely on expected values
  • Risk-seeking investors: Willing to accept lower expected returns for chance of higher gains

Most corporate decision-makers are risk-averse, which is reflected in: - Use of hurdle rates above the cost of capital - Preference for shorter payback periods - Conservative cash flow projections

Glossary of Key Terms

Capital budgeting: The process of planning and managing a company’s long-term investments

Cash flow: The actual movement of money into or out of a business

Conventional cash flows: Cash flow pattern with an initial outflow followed by inflows

Cost of capital: The required return necessary to make a capital budgeting project worthwhile

Discounted cash flow (DCF): A valuation method that estimates the value of an investment based on its expected future cash flows

Discounting: The process of determining the present value of a future cash flow

Discount rate: The interest rate used to convert future cash flows to present value

Hurdle rate: The minimum rate of return required for a project to be acceptable

Incremental cash flow: Additional cash flow that occurs only because of a decision

Internal rate of return (IRR): The discount rate at which the NPV of a project equals zero

Net present value (NPV): The difference between the present value of cash inflows and outflows

Non-conventional cash flows: Cash flows that change sign more than once

Opportunity cost: The value of the next best alternative foregone when making a decision

Payback period: Time required to recover the initial investment from project cash flows

Present value: The current worth of a future cash flow, adjusted for the time value of money

Residual value: The estimated value of an asset at the end of its useful life

Risk-adjusted discount rate: A discount rate that includes a premium for risk

Sensitivity analysis: Technique to determine how changes in variables affect an outcome

Sunk cost: A cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered

Time value of money: The concept that money available now is worth more than the same amount in the future

  • Multiple appraisal methods should be used in combination for a complete perspective, as each provides different insights into the investment opportunity

  • Risk assessment is an essential component of investment decision-making, helping to quantify uncertainties and test the robustness of projections

Real Options in Capital Investment Decisions

Real options analysis recognises that investment decisions often contain embedded options that can significantly impact project value.

Real Options Framework

Real options represent flexibility in decision-making. Unlike financial options (which give the right to buy or sell financial assets), real options give managers the right to make business decisions.

Real Options in Investment
├── Option to Defer
├── Option to Expand
├── Option to Abandon
├── Option to Contract
└── Option to Switch

Real options create value through managerial flexibility to adapt to changing circumstances.

Understanding Real Options Through an Analogy

Analogy: House Construction Project

Think of real options like building a house in stages:

  1. Option to Defer: You buy land but wait to start construction until property prices rise
    • You’ve paid something (land cost) to create future flexibility
  2. Option to Expand: You build extra-deep foundations that would allow adding a second story later
    • You spend more now for the option to expand cheaply later
  3. Option to Abandon: You design the house so it could easily be sold as a commercial property
    • You’ve created an “exit strategy” if residential plans don’t work out
  4. Option to Contract: You design the house with a separate suite that could be rented out
    • You’ve created flexibility to scale back your personal space if needed
  5. Option to Switch: You install systems that allow the house to use either gas or electric heating
    • You’ve created flexibility to switch between energy sources based on prices

Common Real Option Types

Option Type Description Value Driver Business Example
Defer Ability to postpone investment Reduces uncertainty before committing Oil company delaying field development until prices rise
Expand Opportunity to increase scale Captures upside potential E-commerce firm building oversized warehouse for future growth
Abandon Ability to exit unprofitable projects Limits downside risk Pharmaceutical company stopping drug development after poor trial results
Contract Option to reduce project scale Mitigates losses in adverse conditions Airline reducing fleet size during travel downturns
Switch Flexibility to change inputs/outputs Adapts to changing market conditions Manufacturing plant that can produce different products

How Real Options Add Value

Traditional NPV analysis often undervalues projects because it: - Assumes decisions are made now with no future flexibility - Doesn’t account for management’s ability to adapt to new information

Example: Mining Project

Traditional NPV analysis: - Initial investment: £50 million - Expected cash flows (present value): £45 million - NPV: -£5 million (would reject project)

Real options analysis recognises: - Option to delay production if metal prices are low - Option to expand production if prices rise significantly - Option to abandon if costs exceed projections

After accounting for these options, the project might have a positive value despite negative traditional NPV.

Valuation Approaches

Real options can be valued using:

  1. Black-Scholes Model: Originally developed for financial options, can be adapted for simple real options
    • Works best when there’s a clear exercise date and price
  2. Binomial Lattice: Maps out possible future scenarios and decision points
    • Better for complex options with multiple decision points
  3. Monte Carlo Simulation: Uses thousands of random scenarios to estimate option value
    • Best for highly uncertain situations with multiple variables

Practical Example in Stages

A mining company considering a new site can view the investment as a series of options:

Stage 1: Exploration (Option to Defer) - Cost: £1 million for initial exploration - Creates option to develop if resources found - If no resources, lose £1 million - If resources found, move to Stage 2

Stage 2: Development (Option to Expand/Contract) - Cost: £20 million to develop the mine - Creates option to extract at different scales - Can choose small-scale (£10m) or large-scale (£20m) development

Stage 3: Extraction (Option to Switch/Abandon) - Can adjust extraction rate based on mineral prices - Can temporarily halt production if prices fall too low - Can sell the mine if prospects deteriorate permanently

This staged approach allows management to: 1. Limit initial exposure (only £1m at first) 2. Make informed decisions as uncertainty resolves 3. Capture upside potential while limiting downside risk

Profitability Index: A Key Tool for Capital Rationing

When you can’t fund all profitable projects, you need to know which provide the most “bang for your buck.”

Profitability Index (PI) measures the value created per pound invested:

PI = Present Value of Future Cash Flows / Initial Investment

Mathematical breakdown:

PI = ∑[Cash Flow_t / (1+r)^t] / Initial Investment

Where: - t = time period (typically years) - r = discount rate

In simpler terms: - Calculate the present value of all future cash inflows - Divide by the initial investment amount

Interpreting the result: - PI > 1.0: Project creates value (accept if possible) - PI = 1.0: Project breaks even (indifferent) - PI < 1.0: Project destroys value (reject)

Example of Profitability Index Calculation

Consider three projects with capital constraint of £500,000:

Project Initial Investment NPV PI Ranking
A £200,000 £40,000 1.20 2
B £300,000 £90,000 1.30 1
C £250,000 £37,500 1.15 3

Step 1: Calculate PI for each project - Project A: PI = (£200,000 + £40,000) / £200,000 = 1.20 - Project B: PI = (£300,000 + £90,000) / £300,000 = 1.30 - Project C: PI = (£250,000 + £37,500) / £250,000 = 1.15

Step 2: Rank projects by PI (highest first)

Step 3: Allocate capital to highest PI projects until exhausted - Select Project B (£300,000) - Remaining budget: £200,000 - Select Project A (£200,000) - Budget now fully allocated

Result: Projects B and A should be funded, creating a total NPV of £130,000

Mathematical Programming Approach

For complex situations with multiple constraints, linear programming can be used:

Objective function:

Maximise ∑(NPV_j × X_j)

Subject to constraints:

∑(Investment_j × X_j) ≤ Capital Budget
X_j ∈ {0,1} or 0 ≤ X_j ≤ 1 (depending on divisibility)

Where: - X_j represents the proportion of project j undertaken - j ranges from 1 to the number of available projects

This approach is useful when: - Projects can be partially funded - Multiple constraints exist (not just capital) - Projects are interdependent

Example: A company might face constraints on: - Total capital available - Risk exposure per sector - Human resources available - Manufacturing capacity

Linear programming finds the optimal allocation across all constraints.

The Shadow Price Concept Explained

Many ESG factors don’t have market prices but do have real value or cost.

Shadow price: The estimated true economic value of something not fully reflected in market prices

Example: Carbon emissions - Market price might be £0 (no carbon tax) - But emissions create real economic costs through climate change - Shadow price of £50/tonne attempts to capture this true cost - Adding this to financial projections creates more accurate valuation

Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis

When ESG factors are difficult to monetise, a weighted scoring approach can be used:

Project Score = ∑(w_i × Factor Score_i)

Where: - w_i represents the weight assigned to factor i - Factor Score_i is the performance score for factor i

The process: 1. Identify all relevant decision factors 2. Assign weights to each factor based on importance 3. Score each project on each factor (e.g., 1-10 scale) 4. Calculate weighted score 5. Rank projects by total score

Understanding Monte Carlo Simulation

Traditional risk analysis uses a few discrete scenarios (e.g., best, most likely, worst case)

Monte Carlo simulation creates thousands of scenarios by: 1. Identifying key variables (e.g., price, volume, costs) 2. Defining probability distributions for each variable 3. Randomly sampling from these distributions 4. Calculating outcomes for each combination 5. Building a probability distribution of results

Practice Exercises

To consolidate your understanding, attempt these practice problems before tomorrow’s session:

  1. A project requires an initial investment of £200,000 and is expected to generate annual cash flows of £50,000 for 5 years. Calculate the project’s payback period, NPV (using a 12% discount rate), and IRR.

  2. Company A is considering two mutually exclusive projects with the following cash flows:

Year Project X (£) Project Y (£)
0 -300,000 -300,000
1 100,000 250,000
2 150,000 100,000
3 150,000 50,000
4 50,000 25,000

Using a discount rate of 10%, calculate: - The NPV of each project - The IRR of each project - The payback period of each project - Which project should be selected and why?

  1. How would your recommendation for question 2 change if:
    • The company’s cost of capital increased to 15%?
    • The company faced capital rationing and could only invest £200,000?
    • Project Y’s Year 1 cash flow was uncertain and could vary by ±20%?
